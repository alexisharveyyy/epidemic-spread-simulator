The SIR Compartmental Model

Mathematical foundations of epidemic spread simulation.

HISTORICAL CONTEXT

The compartmental model that underpins this simulator was introduced by William Ogilvy Kermack and Anderson Gray McKendrick in their 1927 paper A Contribution to the Mathematical Theory of Epidemics (Proc. R. Soc. Lond. A, 115, 700–721). They proposed that a population could be divided into discrete groups — Susceptible, Infected, and Recovered — with movement between groups governed by a small set of ordinary differential equations.

Despite being nearly a century old, the SIR framework is still the starting point for modern epidemic modeling. Extensions like SEIR (adding an Exposed compartment), agent-based models, and metapopulation models all build on the same compartmental abstraction.

Its enduring value is conceptual: a few well-chosen state variables and rate equations capture the qualitative shape of an epidemic curve — rise, peak, and decline — even when many real-world details are abstracted away.

THE EQUATIONS

$\dfrac{dS}{dt} = -\dfrac{\beta S I}{N}$

Susceptible individuals become infected at a rate proportional to the contact between susceptibles and infecteds.

$\dfrac{dI}{dt} = \dfrac{\beta S I}{N} - \gamma I$

Infected individuals are added from new infections and removed through recovery.

$\dfrac{dR}{dt} = \gamma I$

Recovered (or removed) individuals accumulate at the recovery rate.

PARAMETER INTUITION

BETA (β)

Transmission rate — the per-capita rate at which contacts between susceptibles and infecteds produce new infections. Approximate values from the literature: measles ≈ 0.9, COVID-19 original strain ≈ 0.3–0.4, seasonal influenza ≈ 0.2.

GAMMA (γ)

Recovery rate — the inverse of the mean infectious period. If the average individual is infectious for 7 days, $\gamma \approx 1/7 \approx 0.143$. Faster recovery (larger $\gamma$) shortens outbreaks and lowers $R_0$.

THE REPRODUCTION NUMBER

The basic reproduction number $R_0 = \beta / \gamma$ is the expected number of secondary infections caused by a single infected individual in an entirely susceptible population. The effective reproduction number $R_t = R_0 \cdot S(t)/N$ accounts for the depletion of susceptibles over time.

HERD IMMUNITY THRESHOLD

The fraction of the population that must be immune to prevent sustained transmission is $1 - 1/R_0$. For COVID-19 with $R_0 \approx 2.5$, the threshold is $1 - 1/2.5 = 60\%$. For measles with $R_0 \approx 15$, it climbs to roughly 93%.

WHAT SIR DOES NOT CAPTURE

  • Heterogeneous mixing: real contact networks are not uniform.
  • Age structure: children, adults, and elderly differ in contact and outcome.
  • Vaccination dynamics: SIR has no explicit vaccinated compartment.
  • Waning immunity: recovered individuals stay immune forever in SIR.
  • Multi-strain dynamics: SIR tracks a single pathogen.
  • Behavioral change: $\beta$ is constant in time, ignoring lockdowns or fatigue.
  • Geographic structure: the population is treated as a single well-mixed pool.
