Glossary

Key terms in compartmental epidemic modeling.

Attack rate

Total proportion of the population infected during an outbreak. Equivalent in SIR to the final value of $R(t)/N$ after the epidemic burns out.

Basic reproduction number (R₀)

Expected number of secondary infections produced by a single infected individual in a fully susceptible population. For SIR, $R_0 = \beta / \gamma$.

Compartmental model

A class of epidemic models that divides the population into discrete states (compartments) and tracks transitions between them via differential equations.

Effective reproduction number (Rₜ)

The reproduction number at time $t$, accounting for the depletion of susceptibles. $R_t = R_0 \cdot S(t)/N$. The turning point of the outbreak is when $R_t$ crosses 1.

Force of infection

The per-capita rate at which susceptibles become infected. For SIR, $\lambda(t) = \beta I(t)/N$. It is the instantaneous hazard a susceptible individual faces.

Generation time

The average interval between an infection and the secondary infections it produces. Closely related to the serial interval, which is the gap between symptom onset in primary and secondary cases.

Herd immunity threshold

Fraction of the population that must be immune to prevent sustained transmission. For SIR, $1 - 1/R_0$. Sustained transmission below this threshold requires re-introduction from outside.

Homogeneous mixing

Simplifying assumption that every individual has equal probability of contact with every other individual. Real populations exhibit assortative mixing by age, geography, and household.

Infectious period

Duration over which an infected individual can transmit the pathogen. Mean infectious period in SIR is $1/\gamma$, so a $\gamma$ of 0.1 corresponds to a 10-day infectious period.

SEIR model

Extension of SIR with an Exposed compartment for individuals who are infected but not yet infectious. Used for diseases with a meaningful incubation period such as COVID-19 or measles.

SIR model

Susceptible-Infected-Recovered compartmental model from Kermack & McKendrick (1927); the foundation of modern mathematical epidemiology.

Transmission rate (β)

Rate at which infections occur between contacting susceptible and infected individuals. Combines contact frequency and per-contact transmission probability.

Waning immunity

Gradual loss of acquired immunity over time, requiring SIRS or other extended models. Common for influenza and most coronaviruses; less common for measles.
